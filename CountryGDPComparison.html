A Note on Methodology
What is Purchasing Power Parity?

Nominal GDP measures a country's economic output converted into US dollars at prevailing market exchange rates. It answers the question: how large is this economy in global dollar terms? This is useful for comparing flows of international trade and finance, but it can be misleading when comparing living standards — because a dollar buys very different amounts in different countries.

PPP-adjusted GDP corrects for this by asking: how much would it cost to buy the same basket of goods and services in each country? Economists construct a common price level and re-express each economy in "international dollars," stripping out the distortion of exchange rates. The result better reflects the real productive capacity of an economy and the actual material welfare of its citizens.

A cup of coffee that costs $5 in New York might cost the equivalent of $0.80 in Hanoi. A Vietnamese worker earning $10,000 in nominal terms can therefore afford far more than that figure suggests when measured in local prices. PPP captures this reality — which is why Vietnam's PPP GDP is roughly three times its nominal GDP.
Use Nominal when…
comparing international trade volumes, foreign debt, or the weight of an economy in global markets.
Use PPP when…
comparing living standards, productivity, or the real size of an economy relative to its population.
They converge when…
a country's price level is close to the US average — as in Australia, Switzerland, or the Nordic nations.
